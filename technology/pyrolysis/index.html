Technology / Pyrolysis

Heat without oxygen. Carbon without waste.

Pyrolysis heats biomass and other carbon-rich waste in a sealed, oxygen-free reactor. Because there is no oxygen, nothing burns. The material breaks apart into four valuable streams instead: solid biocarbon, liquid bio-oil, wood vinegar, and a combustible syngas that is fed back to power the process itself. It is the core conversion technology behind most of the Pyroltech product slate.

What is pyrolysis?

Pyrolysis is thermal decomposition in the absence of oxygen. When organic material is heated in air it combusts, releasing its carbon as CO2 and leaving ash. Heat the same material in a sealed reactor with no oxygen and a different chemistry takes over: long carbon chains crack apart, volatile compounds boil off as gases and vapours, and a stable carbon skeleton is left behind. Condense the vapours and you get liquids. Keep the light gases and you get fuel for the reactor. Nothing is wasted, and the carbon that would have gone up the stack stays locked in a solid, sellable form.

The inputs are broad: forestry residues, sawmill waste, agricultural straws and shells, end-of-life wood, tires and other carbon-rich streams. If a material is mostly carbon and reasonably dry, pyrolysis can usually convert it. For wet streams such as biosolids, our hydrothermal route does the same job without a drying step.

Slow or fast pyrolysis: which one, and why?

The same chemistry can be steered toward solids or liquids, and the lever is time and temperature.

Slow pyrolysis uses steadier heating and longer residence times. More of the carbon stays in the solid phase, so the biocarbon fraction is maximised. This is the route when the target is metallurgical biocarbon, activated carbon precursor, or biochar for soil.

Fast pyrolysis heats the material very quickly and quenches the vapours just as fast. That pushes yield toward liquid bio-oil, the feedstock for renewable fuels and chemical intermediates.

Pyroltech does not sell one fixed recipe. We tune temperature, heating rate and residence time to the feedstock you have and the product slate that pays best at your site, and we confirm the split in piloting before anything is built.

How does the plant power itself?

The syngas fraction is the plant's own fuel. At start-up an external burner brings the reactor to temperature; from then on, the gas released by the feedstock is cycled back to sustain the process heat. External energy demand stays minimal in steady operation, which keeps operating cost low and keeps the carbon balance clean. When the feedstock is energy-rich there is surplus heat, and that surplus can be put to work on site.

Frequently asked questions

What is pyrolysis?

Thermal decomposition of biomass and carbon-rich material in the absence of oxygen. Instead of burning to ash, the feedstock splits into biocarbon, bio-oil, wood vinegar and a combustible syngas.

What is the difference between slow and fast pyrolysis?

Slow pyrolysis favours the solid biocarbon fraction through steadier heating and longer residence times. Fast pyrolysis heats quickly to maximise liquid bio-oil. We tune the process to the product slate each project needs.

Does the plant need external fuel?

Only at start-up. In steady operation the syngas produced in the reactor is returned as process heat, so the plant largely powers itself.

Is it proven at commercial scale?

Yes. More than 20 years of operating history across three continents, and a 2 MW plant in Turkey running non-stop today. Contact us for reference details.
