Technology / Hydrothermal

Wet waste, converted wet. No drying step.

Hydrothermal processing converts wet organic waste in hot, pressurised water. The water in the feedstock becomes the reaction medium instead of a cost to remove, so streams like biosolids, sludges, food waste and manures go in as they are. Two routes cover the outputs: hydrothermal carbonization (HTC) yields hydrochar, a coal-like solid, and hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL) yields biocrude, a renewable crude oil. Nutrients concentrate in the process water and are recovered as fertiliser products.

What is hydrothermal processing?

Most thermal conversion technologies want dry feedstock, and that is exactly what many of the largest waste streams are not. Biosolids, sewage sludge, food waste and manures are mostly water, and evaporating that water before conversion consumes so much energy that the economics usually collapse before the chemistry starts.

Hydrothermal processing turns that problem inside out. The feedstock is held in water under pressure and heated; under those conditions water changes character and becomes an active reaction medium that breaks the organic material down. The wetter the stream, the more sense the route makes. There is no dryer in the flowsheet at all.

HTC or HTL: what is the difference?

The two hydrothermal routes are siblings, separated by severity and by what you want out.

Hydrothermal carbonization (HTC) runs at the milder end. Over hours in subcritical water, the organic matter densifies into hydrochar, a solid with coal-like handling and energy density. Hydrochar can serve as a renewable solid fuel, a soil carbon product or a precursor for further carbon upgrading, and the conversion also sanitises the material, which matters for sludges and manures.

Hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL) runs hotter and at higher pressure. Under those conditions the organics break down and re-form as biocrude, a renewable crude oil that can be upgraded and refined toward renewable fuels. HTL is the route when the target is liquid energy rather than solid carbon.

Pyroltech selects the route, and tunes the conditions, based on your feedstock analysis and which product earns most at your site. For dry feedstocks, pyrolysis remains the workhorse; hydrothermal is the specialist for everything wet.

Which feedstocks suit the hydrothermal route?

The short test: if drying it would hurt, hydrothermal fits. Typical streams include:

Biosolids & sewage sludgeMunicipal treatment residuals, converted and sanitised in one step.
Food processing wasteHigh-moisture residues from processors, breweries and dairies.
Manures & digestateFarm and biogas-plant residuals with disposal cost attached.
Seafood processing wasteWet, nutrient-rich residues from fish and shellfish plants.
AlgaeNaturally wet biomass, a strong HTL candidate.
Mixed wet organicsSource-separated organics and other high-moisture streams.

What happens to the nutrients?

Wet waste streams carry more than carbon. Nitrogen and phosphorus, the same nutrients that make sludge and manure a regulatory headache, concentrate in the hydrothermal process water. Rather than treating that water as effluent, Pyroltech recovers the nutrients as fertiliser-grade products, closing the loop on a resource that agriculture needs and that landfills and waterways do not. See nutrient recovery for the product side of this story.

The result is a three-way value stack from a single wet stream: a carbon product (hydrochar or biocrude), recovered nutrients, and the avoided cost of hauling and disposing of the raw waste.

Why does skipping the drying step matter?

Because drying is where wet-waste projects die. Evaporating water takes far more energy than heating it, so any flowsheet that starts with a dryer carries a permanent energy penalty and a permanent cost penalty. Hydrothermal processing keeps the water in the reactor and puts it to work. That is what makes streams like biosolids, which most operators pay to haul away, convertible into products at all.

Frequently asked questions

What is hydrothermal processing?

Conversion of wet organic waste in hot, pressurised water. The water in the feedstock is the reaction medium rather than a problem to remove, so there is no drying step.

What is the difference between HTC and HTL?

HTC runs at milder conditions and produces hydrochar, a coal-like solid. HTL runs hotter and at higher pressure and produces biocrude, a renewable crude oil for refining into fuels.

Which feedstocks suit hydrothermal?

Wet streams that would be uneconomic to dry: biosolids, sewage sludge, food processing waste, manures, digestate, seafood residues and algae.

What happens to the nutrients?

Nitrogen and phosphorus concentrate in the process water and are recovered as fertiliser-grade products, a third revenue stream alongside the carbon products.
