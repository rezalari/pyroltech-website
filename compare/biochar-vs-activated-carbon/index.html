Biochar vs activated carbon.

The short verdict: they are the same family of carbon at two different price points, and the deciding question is whether your job needs engineered adsorption. If the work is soil improvement, bulk carbon or durable carbon storage, plain biochar does it at a lower cost per tonne, with roughly 2.5 tonnes of credit-eligible CO2 removal stacked on each tonne. If the work is purifying water, air or gas, activation opens the internal surface area that adsorption demands, and buyers in that market pay accordingly.

When is plain biochar enough?

Whenever nothing in the spec asks for adsorption capacity. In soil, biochar's porosity holds water and nutrients, improves structure and supports microbial life, and none of that requires an activated pore network. The same is true when the goal is simply to lock carbon away: the durable storage is what earns the credit, at roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne, and paying for activation would add cost without adding a single credit. For farms, growers, land restoration and carbon projects, biochar is the right tool at the right price.

When does activation pay?

When a buyer needs contaminants pulled out of a fluid and held. Activation opens an internal surface area that plain char cannot match, which is why activated carbon is the working heart of drinking-water treatment, wastewater polishing, odour control and gas cleanup. Those duties come with performance specs, and customers pay a multiple of biochar prices to meet them, in a global market around 5.6 billion US dollars today and heading toward roughly 11.9 billion by 2034. If your application has an adsorption spec, activation is not a luxury, it is the product.

Do you have to choose just one?

No, and the strongest projects usually do not. Activation is a downstream step on the same pyrolysis platform, so one plant can sell biochar into soil and carbon-storage markets while upgrading part of its output to activated carbon for a water or air customer nearby. Pyroltech configures that split around the buyers you actually have, not the other way around.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between biochar and activated carbon?

Both are porous carbons from pyrolysis. Activated carbon gets an extra activation step that opens far more internal surface area for adsorption; biochar skips it, costs less, and serves soil and carbon-storage jobs.

When is plain biochar the right choice?

For soil amendment, water and nutrient retention, bulk carbon and durable storage. It is cheaper per tonne and its stored carbon is credit-eligible at ~2.5 t CO2 per tonne.

When does activation pay for itself?

When the spec demands engineered adsorption: water, air and gas purification. That market is ~US$5.6B today, growing toward ~US$11.9B by 2034.

Can one plant make both?

Yes. Activation is a downstream step on the same platform, so output can be split between soil-grade biochar and purification-grade activated carbon.
