Pyrolysis vs gasification.

The short verdict: choose the route by the product you want to sell, not by the technology's reputation. Pyrolysis heats material without oxygen, so the carbon survives as saleable biocarbon alongside bio-oil and gas, and that surviving carbon is what earns durable removal credits. Gasification runs hotter with controlled oxygen, deliberately consuming the solids to maximise syngas for hydrogen, fuels and power. Carbon products and credits point to pyrolysis; gas volume points to gasification; many projects sensibly use both.

When is pyrolysis the right route?

When the carbon in your feedstock is worth more as a product than as a fuel. Forestry residues, agricultural waste, end-of-life tires and biosolids all carry carbon that pyrolysis preserves as biocarbon, activated carbon or recovered carbon black, with bio-oil and wood vinegar alongside. Just as important, only carbon that survives can be certified as durable removal, so if credits are part of your business case, pyrolysis is the route that keeps them on the table.

When is gasification the right route?

When the target is molecules, not solids. If the project exists to make hydrogen, methanol, synthetic fuels or on-site power, gasification's job is to push as much of the feedstock as possible into clean syngas, and it does that job well, especially on mixed or lower-grade streams where no premium solid product is waiting at the other end. The trade is explicit: more gas, no solid carbon, no removal credit.

Why not both?

Often the best flowsheet is sequential. Pyrolysis takes the first pass and pulls out the high-value solids and liquids; the gas fraction is then cleaned, upgraded or gasified further for hydrogen or power. One plant, two product families, and the process heat loops back into the reactor. Frequently asked questions

What is the fundamental difference?

Oxygen. Pyrolysis heats material with none, so the carbon survives as solid biocarbon plus liquids and gas. Gasification runs hotter with controlled oxygen, consuming the solids to maximise syngas.

Which route should a project choose?

Choose by product. Solid carbon products and removal credits point to pyrolysis; hydrogen, synthetic fuels and power point to gasification.

Can the two routes be combined?

Yes. A common configuration is pyrolysis first for the solids and liquids, then upgrading or gasifying the gas fraction, so one plant captures both product families.
