How is waste converted?

Pyrolysis

Pyrolysis is the heating of organic material in the absence of oxygen so that it decomposes instead of burning. The result is a stable solid carbon (biochar or biocarbon), condensable liquids (bio-oil and wood vinegar) and a fuel gas (syngas).

It is the workhorse of the Pyroltech platform because it keeps carbon in solid, saleable form. Read about our pyrolysis technology.

Gasification

Gasification converts carbon-rich material into syngas at high temperature using a controlled amount of oxygen or steam. Where pyrolysis maximises solid carbon, gasification maximises gas for power and fuels.

We use it where the priority is energy output rather than solid product. See gasification and upgrading, or compare the two in pyrolysis vs gasification.

HTC (hydrothermal carbonization)

Hydrothermal carbonization converts wet biomass into a coal-like solid called hydrochar using hot, pressurized water. Because the water does the work, the feedstock never needs energy-intensive drying.

That makes HTC the right tool for sludges, biosolids and food waste. Read about our hydrothermal processes.

HTL (hydrothermal liquefaction)

Hydrothermal liquefaction converts wet biomass into biocrude, a crude-oil-like liquid, at higher temperature and pressure than HTC. Like HTC, it accepts wet feedstocks directly.

HTL is the wet-waste route into the fuels value chain. See how HTL fits our hydrothermal platform.

What are the solid carbon products?

Biochar

Biochar is the stable, porous solid carbon produced when biomass is pyrolysed. Applied to soil it improves water and nutrient retention, and the carbon it contains stays out of the atmosphere for centuries.

That durability is why biochar earns carbon-removal credits, roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne. See biochar as a soil amendment.

Biocarbon

Biocarbon is engineered, industrial-grade renewable carbon from pyrolysis, produced to a specification rather than for soil. It is the family name for the solid carbons we tune for steel, filtration and other industrial uses.

In steelmaking it can replace 20-30% of fossil coke without process redesign. See the biocarbon product line.

Hydrochar

Hydrochar is the energy-dense solid produced by hydrothermal carbonization of wet feedstocks. It handles and stores like a dry fuel even though it started as sludge or slurry.

It turns disposal liabilities such as biosolids into a usable carbon product. Read about HTC and hydrochar.

Metallurgical coke (met coke)

Met coke is fossil carbon made by baking coking coal, used as the reductant and fuel in iron and steel making. It is the benchmark biocarbon competes against, typically trading around $240-290 per tonne.

Replacing part of it with biogenic carbon is one of steel's fastest decarbonization levers. Compare biocarbon vs met coke.

PCI (pulverized coal injection)

PCI is the practice of injecting finely ground coal into a blast furnace to reduce how much coke the furnace consumes. It is one of the easiest places to introduce renewable carbon, because the injected material is already a powder fed through existing equipment.

Biocarbon drops into PCI streams at 20-30% substitution. See biocarbon for steel and iron.

rCB (recovered carbon black)

Recovered carbon black is carbon black reclaimed from end-of-life tires through pyrolysis. It substitutes virgin fossil carbon black in new rubber and plastic compounds.

At around 20% substitution the addressable market is roughly EUR 3.2 billion. See recovered carbon black.

What liquids, gases and nutrients come out?

Syngas

Syngas is a fuel gas made mostly of hydrogen and carbon monoxide, produced by gasification and pyrolysis. It can run engines and turbines directly or be upgraded into hydrogen and liquid fuels.

In our plants it is also what powers the process itself. See clean gases.

Biocrude

Biocrude is a renewable, crude-oil-like liquid produced by hydrothermal liquefaction of wet biomass. Refineries can co-process or upgrade it into finished fuels using existing assets.

It is the bridge between wet waste streams and the fuel pool. See bio-oil and biocrude.

SAF (sustainable aviation fuel)

Sustainable aviation fuel is drop-in jet fuel made from non-fossil feedstocks. It blends into existing aircraft, engines and airport fuel systems without modification.

Waste-derived intermediates such as biocrude and syngas are recognised routes to SAF. See sustainable aviation fuel.

Wood vinegar

Wood vinegar, also called pyroligneous acid, is the aqueous condensate collected when biomass is pyrolysed. Farmers use it as a natural input for plant vigour, composting and pest management.

It turns a process side-stream into an agricultural product. See wood vinegar.

Struvite

Struvite is magnesium ammonium phosphate, a crystalline nutrient recovered from streams such as wastewater and digestate. It is sold as a slow-release phosphorus fertilizer.

Recovering it protects waterways and closes the nutrient loop. See nutrient recovery.

How is the carbon counted and credited?

Carbon-removal credit

A carbon-removal credit certifies that one tonne of CO2 has been physically removed from the atmosphere and stored durably. It is different from an offset, which usually pays to avoid an emission elsewhere.

Biochar carbon removal is an IPCC-recognised pathway, and removal-credit indexes have recently ranged around $130-165 per tonne of CO2. See carbon-removal credits.

CORC

A CORC, or CO2 Removal Certificate, is the unit issued by the Puro.earth registry for one verified tonne of durable carbon removal. Biochar projects are among the most common sources of issued CORCs.

It is the certificate form in which our removals typically reach buyers. See how credits are generated and sold.

Scope 1 and Scope 3 emissions

Scope 1 emissions are the direct emissions from a company's own operations; Scope 3 emissions are those across its value chain, including purchased materials and sold products. A steel mill's coke use is Scope 1 for the mill and Scope 3 for everyone who buys its steel.

Replacing fossil carbon with biocarbon can cut a mill's Scope 1 CO2 by more than 30% at full substitution potential, which flows through as lower Scope 3 for its customers. See carbon removal and ESG for buyers.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between biochar and biocarbon?

Both are stable solid carbon from pyrolysis. Biochar usually means grades applied to soil, while biocarbon means engineered industrial grades produced to a specification, for example replacing 20-30% of met coke in steelmaking.

What is the difference between pyrolysis and gasification?

Pyrolysis heats material with no oxygen and keeps much of the carbon as a solid product. Gasification adds controlled oxygen or steam and converts most of the carbon into syngas for power or fuels.

Is a carbon-removal credit the same as a carbon offset?

No. Most offsets pay to avoid an emission somewhere else. A removal credit certifies that a tonne of CO2 was physically taken out of the atmosphere and stored durably, as biochar carbon removal does.
