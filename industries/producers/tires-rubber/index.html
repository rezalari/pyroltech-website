Why are scrap tires such a stubborn problem?

Tires are engineered not to break down, which is exactly what makes them hard to dispose of. Stockpiles are fire hazards, landfills refuse or surcharge them, and shredding for playgrounds and fuel destroys most of the material value while microplastic and combustion concerns close those outlets down. Regulators keep raising the cost of doing the wrong thing with tires, and generators, haulers and stewardship programs are paying real money for a better answer.

What does a tonne of tires become?

Continuous pyrolysis takes the tire apart into the materials it was made from. The rubber cracks into recovered carbon black and tire-derived oil, the steel cord comes out clean for the scrap market, and the gas fraction is used to power the process itself, so the plant largely runs on its own byproduct.

How do the economics stack?

Tire pyrolysis earns on both sides of the plant gate. On the way in, you are paid to take a waste that generators must, by regulation, send somewhere responsible. On the way out, the same tonne sells three times: rCB, oil and steel. The gate fee de-risks the intake, the products carry the upside, and the process gas trims the energy bill. Few waste streams pay twice like this, and it is why continuous tire lines anchor many of the strongest waste-to-value business cases we see.

Where is this running today?

The technology is commercial, not conceptual. Pyroltech's team has built waste-to-value plants for more than 20 years across three continents, and the core system runs today at a 2 MW plant in Turkey, running non-stop. Ask us for the details.

Why now?

Tire makers have made public recycled-content commitments, and recovered carbon black is the material that lets them keep those promises, which is what puts the addressable rCB market at roughly 3.2 billion euros at around 20 percent substitution of virgin carbon black. At the same time, disposal rules on tires keep tightening. The feedstock side pays more and the product side pays more, and continuous pyrolysis is the bridge between them.

Frequently asked questions

What comes out of a tonne of tires?

Recovered carbon black, tire-derived oil, clean steel cord for scrap, and a gas fraction that powers the process itself.

How do the economics work?

You are paid a gate fee to take the waste, then the same tonne pays again as rCB, oil and steel, with process gas offsetting energy costs.

Is recovered carbon black a real market?

Yes. At about 20 percent substitution of virgin carbon black, the addressable market is roughly EUR 3.2 billion, pulled by tire makers' recycled-content commitments.

Is the process batch or continuous?

Continuous. The system is engineered for steady, round-the-clock throughput with consistent product quality.

Where is the technology proven?

At a 2 MW plant in Turkey, running non-stop, built by a team with 20+ years of plants across three continents. Ask us for details.
