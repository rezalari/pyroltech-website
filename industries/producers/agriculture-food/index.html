Why is organic residue a growing problem?

Agriculture and food processing generate residue at every step: straw and stover in the field, shells and husks at the sheller and mill, pomace at the press, spent grain at the brewery and distillery, trim and off-spec material on the processing line. Field burning is restricted in more places every year, landfill space for organics is closing, and hauling wet, bulky residue is expensive. Most operations are paying to make the problem go away, season after season.

What do these residues become?

Pyrolysis converts the dry and fibrous fraction into biochar, a stable, porous carbon that improves water retention and nutrient holding in soil, plus wood vinegar, a liquid co-product used in crop and soil applications, plus heat that can dry feedstock or serve process loads. Nutrients concentrated in certain streams can be recovered rather than lost. Everything that arrives as waste leaves as something with a use.

How does the value come back to the operation?

Three ways. First, avoided disposal: the residue you were paying to haul, tip or manage becomes feedstock. Second, products: biochar can be used on your own land, sold into horticulture and landscaping, or blended into growing media, while wood vinegar sells into crop and soil applications. Third, carbon: the stable carbon locked in biochar is certifiable as durable removal under an IPCC-recognised pathway, at roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of biocarbon, a revenue stream that stacks on the product itself.

Where is this running today?

The technology is commercial, not conceptual. Pyroltech's team has built waste-to-value plants for more than 20 years across three continents, and the core system runs today at a 2 MW plant in Turkey, running non-stop. Ask us for the details.

Why now?

Organics bans and field-burning restrictions are tightening, fertilizer and water costs keep rising, and food buyers increasingly ask growers and processors about their footprint. Biochar answers all three at once: it removes a disposal liability, improves soil performance where water and nutrients are the constraint, and produces a measurable, durable carbon story that a commodity residue never had.

Frequently asked questions

What wastes can you process?

Crop residues such as straw and stover, nut shells, rice and grain husks, fruit pomace, spent grain and other brewery and distillery byproducts, and dry organic streams from food processing.

What do the residues become?

Biochar for soil and horticulture, wood vinegar for crop and soil applications, and process heat or energy for the facility that produced the waste.

Can the biochar go back onto our own land?

Yes. Many operators use it on their own soils, sell the surplus, and certify the durable carbon as removal credits.

Does this replace composting or digestion?

It complements them. Pyrolysis suits the dry, woody and fibrous fraction; wet streams stay better matched to digestion or hydrothermal processing.

Is there a carbon-revenue angle?

Yes. Biochar carbon is credit-eligible under an IPCC-recognised removal pathway, roughly 2.5 t CO2 per tonne of biocarbon, on top of product sales.
