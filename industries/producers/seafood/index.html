Why is seafood waste such a stubborn problem?

Because it is wet, it spoils in hours and it usually lands in places with the fewest disposal options. Coastal and island processors face long hauls or barge runs to landfill, rising gate fees, tightening rules on ocean disposal and neighbours with a low tolerance for odour. Composting struggles with shell volumes, and the calcium that makes shells a nuisance in a landfill is exactly what makes them valuable in a kiln.

What does pyrolysis turn it into?

Heat without oxygen converts the organic fraction into stable carbon while the shell minerals remain, yielding a calcium-rich biochar. In soil it acts as a liming agent and carbon amendment in one pass, raising pH on acidic ground while improving water and nutrient retention. In water treatment it works as a filtration and adsorption medium, with the mineral fraction helping bind phosphates and metals. The process also kills pathogens and eliminates the odour problem at its source.

Is this proven technology?

The conversion platform is already running. Pyroltech's pyrolysis technology operates today at a 2 MW plant in Turkey, running non-stop, backed by more than 20 years of thermochemical plant-building across three continents. Shell and residue streams are matched to that platform through a feedstock characterization study. Ask us and we will walk you through the reference plant and the seafood-specific work.

Why does this fit coastal communities now?

Because the economics of hauling waste off an island or up a coast only get worse, while the products biochar replaces, agricultural lime, imported soil amendments and filtration media, usually arrive on the same expensive barge going the other way. Converting locally closes that loop: the waste stays, the value stays, and the biogenic carbon locked into the biochar is credit-eligible under the IPCC-recognised biochar pathway, a second revenue line for the community that owns the unit.

Frequently asked questions

What seafood waste can be converted?

Crab, shrimp, lobster, oyster and mussel shells, fish offcuts and aquaculture residues. Shell-heavy streams yield calcium-rich biochar; mixed streams are blended and assessed in a study.

What is calcium-rich biochar good for?

Soil, as a combined liming agent and carbon amendment, and water treatment, as a filtration and adsorption medium where the mineral fraction helps bind phosphates and metals.

Does this work for a small coastal plant?

Yes, that is the design case. Compact modular units suit a single plant or a shared community facility, so waste is converted where it lands.

Is there carbon-credit value?

The biogenic residue converted to stable biocarbon is credit-eligible under the IPCC-recognised biochar pathway, indicatively around 2.5 t CO2 per tonne of biocarbon, certified per project.
