Why is residual waste getting more expensive?

Landfill space is finite, diversion targets keep rising and gate fees follow. Meanwhile the hardest fraction keeps growing: films, multi-layer packaging and contaminated mixed plastics that no sorting line can save, plus the residual MSW left after recyclables are pulled. Exporting the problem is closing off as an option, and every year a municipality waits, the same tonnes cost more to bury and haul further to do it.

What does conversion turn it into?

Thermochemical conversion, gasification with upgrading for mixed streams and pyrolysis for plastics-rich streams, breaks the material down into three sellable fractions instead of one buried liability. Clean gases power the process or feed further upgrading. The oil fraction can return to the chemical industry as circular feedstock for new plastics, closing the loop mechanical recycling cannot. The carbon fraction serves industrial uses. The split is tuned to what your region can sell.

Why do the diversion economics work now?

Because the floor of the business case is money you already spend. Avoided tipping and hauling anchor the model, product revenue from gas, oil and carbon sits on top, and policy keeps moving one way: diversion targets, extended producer responsibility and chemical-industry commitments to recycled content all raise the value of every diverted tonne. A conversion facility turns a rising cost curve into a revenue line the municipality or hauler controls.

Frequently asked questions

What waste streams qualify?

Residual MSW after recyclables are pulled, sorted mixed plastics including films and multi-layer packaging, and commercial and industrial residues. Each stream is characterized before sizing.

What do the outputs become?

Clean gases for process energy or upgrading, an oil fraction usable as circular feedstock for chemicals and new plastics, and a solid carbon fraction. The split is tuned to feedstock and market.

How is this different from incineration?

Incineration burns everything for one pass of heat and leaves ash. Conversion runs with little or no oxygen and makes products, including molecules the chemical industry can reuse.
