Why is sludge so expensive to get rid of?

Because you are paying to move water. Dewatered sludge is still largely water, and every tonne of it carries hauling, gate fees and long-run liability. The traditional outlets are all under pressure at once: landfills are tightening on organics, incineration is costly and unpopular, and land application faces growing scrutiny over contaminants. For most utilities and plants, sludge is the disposal cost that only moves in one direction.

How does hydrothermal processing change the equation?

Hydrothermal carbonization (HTC) and hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL) run in hot, pressurized water, so the moisture that makes sludge a problem everywhere else is the reaction medium here. Wet feedstock goes straight in from the dewatering line. Out the other side come a solid hydrochar, an upgradeable biocrude on the HTL route, and a concentrated stream from which phosphorus and nitrogen can be recovered as fertilizer feedstock. The same heat and pressure destroy pathogens along the way.

Frequently asked questions

Do we have to dry the sludge first?

No. HTC and HTL work in hot pressurized water, so wet sludge goes in as it comes off the dewatering line. The water is the reaction medium, not a problem to evaporate away.

What do we get out of our sludge?

Three value streams: hydrochar, a solid carbon product; biocrude, an upgradeable liquid on the HTL route; and recovered nutrients, with phosphorus and nitrogen captured as fertilizer feedstock.

What happens to contaminants and pathogens?

The process conditions destroy pathogens and break down many organic contaminants, one reason utilities look at hydrothermal routes as land-application scrutiny tightens. Specific contaminant fate is assessed per feedstock in a characterization study.

With a paid characterization study: we analyze your sludge, model yields and size a system against your current disposal cost, so the business case is on paper before any hardware is.
