Why is avoidance no longer enough?

The credibility of corporate climate claims now rests on the quality of the tonnes behind them. Cheap avoidance offsets, credits for emissions that supposedly would have happened, have been challenged repeatedly, and buyers who relied on them have paid for it in audits, headlines and restated targets. Net-zero frameworks increasingly require that residual emissions be neutralised with durable removals, carbon physically taken out of the cycle and kept out. That shift leaves ESG teams with a sourcing problem: durable removal supply is scarce, and much of it is either very expensive or very early-stage.

What are you actually buying?

A biochar removal credit is backed by a physical tonne of biocarbon. Biomass carbon that would have returned to the atmosphere through decay or burning is converted by pyrolysis into a stable, mineral-like carbon and placed into long-lived uses. The batch is weighed, its carbon content is analysed, the durable fraction is calculated under a registered methodology, and a third party certifies the credit. Roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2 are locked per tonne of biocarbon. It is one of the few removal pathways that is IPCC-recognised, deliverable today and priced well below engineered options.

Why contract now?

Durable removal demand is compounding as net-zero deadlines approach, while certified supply grows slowly, and biochar has delivered the large majority of durable removals to date. Buyers who contract forward secure volume and price before the queue lengthens; buyers who wait compete for what is left at spot. A modest multi-year offtake, sized to your residual-emissions forecast, locks in auditable tonnes at a known cost and signals seriousness to your own stakeholders.

Frequently asked questions

How is a biochar removal credit different from an offset?

Most offsets are avoidance claims: a promise that an emission did not happen. A biochar credit is a removal: carbon headed back to the atmosphere is locked into centuries-stable biocarbon. The tonne exists physically and can be weighed, sampled and audited.

How durable is it?

Biocarbon persists for centuries. Durability is quantified per project under the certification methodology, and biochar carbon removal is an IPCC-recognised pathway.

How are removals measured and verified?

Each batch is weighed and analysed for carbon content, the durable fraction is calculated under a registered methodology, and third-party certification issues the credits, about 2.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of biocarbon.

What do the credits cost?

The biochar credit index has recently traded around $130 to 165 per tonne of CO2, well below engineered options. Specific pricing is set by volume and term in an offtake conversation.

Can we contract future volumes?

Yes. Spot as certified, or multi-year forward offtake tied to production, which is how most serious CDR buyers secure supply and price.
