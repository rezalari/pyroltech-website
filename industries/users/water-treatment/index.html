What is the problem with today's treatment carbon?

Most activated carbon in service is made from mined coal, so a plant built to clean water starts by consuming a fossil resource with a heavy footprint of its own. At the same time, treatment targets keep tightening: metals limits, micropollutants, disinfection by-products and nutrient discharge caps all push utilities and industrial operators toward more adsorption capacity, not less. Buying more of the same coal-based media solves the water problem while quietly worsening the carbon one.

What do we supply instead?

Two complementary media from the same platform. Activated carbon, produced from biomass and activated to the surface area and pore structure your specification calls for, handles dissolved organics, taste and odour, chlorination by-products and many micropollutants. Engineered biochar, a lower-cost media with strong affinity for heavy metals and nutrients, handles lead, cadmium, copper, phosphorus and ammonium capture. Together they cover most of the adsorption duties in a modern plant.

Where does the media come from?

The same pyrolysis platform that runs today at a 2 MW plant in Turkey, running non-stop, converts wood, agricultural and other biomass residues into carbon, then activation tailors that carbon for adsorption duty. The team behind it has built thermal conversion plants for more than twenty years across three continents. Ask us for reference details and sample data for your water chemistry.

Can the loop actually close?

Yes, and this is where water treatment gets interesting. Treatment plants produce biosolids; pyrolysis turns those biosolids into carbon; that carbon goes back into treatment as adsorption media. Nutrient-loaded biochar from phosphorus capture can, subject to loading and local rules, be redirected to land as a slow-release soil input instead of going to landfill. A utility can move from buying fossil media and paying to dispose of residuals toward a loop where its own waste streams supply part of its treatment capacity.

Why switch now?

Demand for adsorption media is growing, with the activated carbon market at roughly US$5.6 billion and heading toward roughly $11.9 billion by 2034, while coal-based supply faces the same decarbonization pressure as every other fossil input. Switching a filter bed is a low-risk decision: the media either meets your specification in trials or it does not. Starting with one duty, taste and odour or metals polishing, lets you validate performance before committing plant-wide.

Frequently asked questions

What can activated carbon and biochar remove from water?

Activated carbon adsorbs dissolved organics, taste and odour compounds, chlorination by-products and many micropollutants. Engineered biochar targets heavy metals such as lead, cadmium and copper, and captures nutrients like phosphorus and ammonium. They are often used together, matched to the stream.

How does biomass-based carbon compare to coal-based?

Same adsorption job, different source. It is made from recovered biomass instead of mined coal, so the media carries a far lower footprint, and activation is tuned to reach the surface area and pore structure your spec calls for.

What happens to spent biochar media?

Nutrient-loaded biochar can, subject to loading and local rules, go to land as a slow-release soil input rather than landfill. Media loaded with metals or persistent organics is managed as a controlled residual.

Can you match our filtration spec?

Yes. Feedstock, pyrolysis and activation are tuned to your iodine number, particle size, hardness and ash limits, granular or powdered, and confirmed with samples before volume supply.
