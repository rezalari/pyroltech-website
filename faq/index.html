How does the technology work?

What is pyrolysis?

Heating organic material in the absence of oxygen, so instead of burning it decomposes into three useful streams: a stable solid carbon (biocarbon), condensable liquids such as bio-oil and wood vinegar, and clean combustible gases. The split is tuned by temperature, residence time and feedstock. The full picture is on our pyrolysis page.

What feedstocks can you process?

Forestry residue and wildfire fuel, agricultural and food-processing byproducts, end-of-life tires and rubber, biosolids and sludge, seafood shells, and sorted municipal plastics. Every feedstock is characterized in the lab first, because moisture, ash and contaminants decide the economics.

Is the technology proven at commercial scale?

Yes. The team has been building waste-to-value plants for more than 20 years across three continents, and the current reference is a 2 MW plant in Turkey, running non-stop. Ask us and we will walk you through the details in a call.

What products come out of the process?

Depending on feedstock and configuration: biocarbon including metallurgical grade, activated carbon, biochar for soil, recovered carbon black, wood vinegar, bio-oil and biocrude, and clean gases. On top of the physical products, the durable carbon is eligible for carbon-removal credits.

What do the products earn?

How much metallurgical coke can biocarbon replace?

Typically 20 to 30 percent without process redesign, across blast-furnace injection, coke blends, sinter and EAF charge carbon. At full substitution the Scope 1 CO2 cut potential exceeds 30 percent. With met coke benchmarked around $240-290 per tonne, displaced coke is real money, not just a compliance line.

How do carbon-removal credits work?

Pyrolysis locks biomass carbon into a form that stays stable for centuries. That durable storage is certifiable as carbon dioxide removal, roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of biocarbon, under an IPCC-recognised pathway. The credit index has recently traded around $130-165 per tonne of CO2, and the credit stacks on top of the product sale.

What other markets do the products serve?

Suitable biocarbon can be upgraded to activated carbon, a market around US$5.6 billion today and growing toward roughly $11.9 billion by 2034. Tire pyrolysis yields recovered carbon black, with an addressable market around EUR3.2 billion at roughly 20 percent substitution in new rubber. Biochar and wood vinegar serve agriculture and soil.

Do you get paid to take problem waste?

Often, yes. For hazardous or hard-to-dispose streams, the fee the owner already pays for disposal becomes plant revenue. In British Columbia, tipping fees for treated wood run roughly $110-170 per tonne before hauling. A plant that destroys the problem and recovers carbon can capture part of that as a gate fee.

How do we work together?

Do you sell equipment, or build and operate plants?

Both, depending on the project. We can supply and commission a system for your site, build-own-operate a plant on or near your waste stream, or form a joint venture where feedstock, capital and operations are shared. The structure follows the project.

What does a first project look like?

Small and deliberate. Feedstock characterization and a paid feasibility study come first, quantifying your volumes, products and economics. If the numbers hold, we move to a pilot, then scale. We would rather execute a small project brilliantly and grow from proof than start with a grand projection.

Where has the team built before?

The team's experience was built across three continents: West Asia and the Middle East, from Turkey to Saudi Arabia and the UAE; Europe, from the Netherlands to Germany and Sweden; and East Asia, across Korea, Japan and China; now deployed in North America.

Do you work with universities and researchers?

Yes. We partner with research groups on feedstock characterization, product development and independent verification, and we can act as the industrial partner or in-kind contributor on grant-funded research. See research partners.
