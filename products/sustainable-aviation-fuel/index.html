Drop-in jet fuel, made from waste carbon.

Pyroltech turns waste carbon into sustainable aviation fuel. Bio-oil from pyrolysis and biocrude from hydrothermal liquefaction are upgraded to jet-range hydrocarbons through two proven synthesis routes, gasification followed by Fischer-Tropsch, or the methanol-to-jet pathway. The result is a drop-in blendstock that mixes into conventional jet fuel with no change to aircraft or airport infrastructure, and it gives airlines and their corporate customers a real path to the Scope reductions they have already promised.

How is waste turned into jet fuel?

The chain starts with thermochemistry. Dry residues go through pyrolysis to bio-oil; wet wastes go through hydrothermal liquefaction to a denser biocrude. From those intermediates, two routes lead to the jet range. In the first, the intermediate is gasified to clean syngas and rebuilt into long-chain paraffins by Fischer-Tropsch synthesis, then hydrocracked and fractionated to jet cut. In the second, syngas is converted to methanol and the methanol-to-jet route assembles it into aviation-range molecules. Both end the same way: hydroprocessing to a stable, water-clear synthetic blendstock.

Why is the jet specification so demanding?

Jet fuel has one of the strictest specifications of any commodity fuel, because it must perform identically at altitude, at temperature extremes and in every engine in the fleet. For waste-derived fuel the hard part is heteroatoms. Biocrudes, especially from wet feedstocks, carry nitrogen and oxygen that fossil kerosene does not, so the process is designed around deep nitrogen removal: hydrotreatment of the intermediate, rigorous syngas cleanup ahead of synthesis, and final hydroprocessing. Getting nitrogen to trace levels is what separates a fuel programme from a chemistry experiment, and it is engineered into every step of our chain.

Why does aviation need waste-based SAF?

Long-haul aviation cannot electrify on any near horizon, so lower-carbon liquid fuel is the sector's practical lever. Blending mandates are arriving in major jurisdictions, airlines carry public Scope commitments, and their corporate customers increasingly buy SAF certificates to address travel emissions. Waste-based SAF answers all three without competing with food crops for land, because the carbon comes from residues that were headed for landfill, burn pile or lagoon.

Frequently asked questions

How is waste turned into jet fuel?

Pyrolysis and hydrothermal liquefaction produce bio-oil and biocrude. These are converted via gasification and Fischer-Tropsch, or via methanol-to-jet, then hydroprocessed into a drop-in jet blendstock.

Is the fuel truly drop-in?

Yes. The target is a synthetic blendstock that blends into conventional jet fuel under established aviation standards, with no change to aircraft, engines or airport fuelling.

Why the emphasis on deep nitrogen removal?

Aviation fuel has a very strict spec, and waste biocrudes carry nitrogen that fossil kerosene does not. Hydrotreatment and syngas cleanup drive it to trace levels before synthesis.

Why should airlines source waste-based SAF?

Aviation cannot electrify long-haul soon, so lower-carbon liquid fuel is the practical lever. Waste-based SAF avoids food-crop competition and serves airline and corporate Scope commitments.
