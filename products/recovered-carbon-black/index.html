Recovered carbon black, reclaimed from end-of-life tires.

Recovered carbon black (rCB) is the carbon filler Pyroltech reclaims from end-of-life tires through pyrolysis. De-metalled and milled to a consistent grade, it replaces a share of virgin carbon black in new tires, technical rubber goods, inks and coatings. And it never comes alone: the same tire also yields saleable pyrolysis oil and steel, so one waste stream becomes three products.

What is recovered carbon black?

Roughly a quarter of a tire's weight is carbon black, the reinforcing filler that gives rubber its strength and colour. When end-of-life tires are pyrolysed, that carbon survives as a solid char. Pyroltech separates the embedded steel cord magnetically, then mills and classifies the char into a fine, consistent powder: recovered carbon black. It is a semi-reinforcing filler that carries the original blacks plus the mineral ash of the tire compound, which is why it is qualified per application rather than sold as a copy of a single virgin grade.

Where does rCB work?

rCB is already used commercially wherever a compound does not demand the very highest reinforcement, and blending lets it move into more demanding recipes step by step.

Why does the economics work?

A scrap tire is a liability for whoever holds it, and tipping or stewardship fees usually pay to take it in. Pyrolysis then turns that paid-for feedstock into three saleable streams: rCB into rubber, plastics and pigment markets, tire-derived oil into fuel and refinery channels, and clean steel scrap into the metals trade. At around 20 percent substitution of virgin carbon black, the rCB opportunity alone is on the order of EUR 3.2 billion, and every tonne of rCB used is a tonne of furnace-process virgin black that does not have to be made from fossil feedstock.

Frequently asked questions

What is rCB and how is it different from virgin carbon black?

rCB is the carbon reclaimed when end-of-life tires are pyrolysed, then de-metalled and milled into a fine powder. It carries the tire's original blacks plus mineral ash, so it acts as a semi-reinforcing filler and is qualified per application rather than sold as a one-to-one copy of a virgin grade.

What substitution rate is realistic?

Around 20 percent in many tire and rubber compounds, and higher in less demanding uses such as mats, mouldings, inks and coatings. At roughly 20 percent substitution the addressable market is about EUR 3.2 billion.

What co-products come out of the same tire?

Tire-derived pyrolysis oil, sold as fuel or refinery feedstock, and the steel cord, separated during de-metalling and sold as scrap. One tire, three revenue streams.

Is rCB lower-carbon than virgin black?

Yes, cradle to gate. Virgin carbon black is made by burning fossil feedstock in a furnace process; rCB reuses carbon that already exists in the tire and keeps that tire out of landfill and open burning.
