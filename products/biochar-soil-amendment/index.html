Biochar that pays the soil back for centuries.

Soil-grade biochar is porous, stable carbon made for the root zone. It holds water and nutrients where plants can reach them, gives soil microbes a permanent home, and keeps its carbon in the ground for centuries instead of a season. Because that storage is durable and measurable, every tonne applied also earns credit-eligible carbon removal, roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2, on top of what it does for the field.

What does biochar do in soil?

Three things, all driven by the same porous structure. First, it acts like a sponge, holding irrigation water and rainfall in the root zone and releasing it as soil dries, which matters most on sandy soils and in dry years. Second, its surfaces hold onto nutrients that would otherwise leach past the roots, so applied fertiliser works harder and less of it ends up in waterways. Third, the pore network is habitat: bacteria and mycorrhizal fungi colonise it and stay, building the underground biology that healthy soil runs on. Unlike compost, which is consumed in a few seasons, the biochar itself persists, so the benefit compounds year over year.

How should it be applied?

Biochar works best charged, not raw. Blending it with compost, manure or a nutrient solution before application loads its surfaces so it starts giving from day one instead of borrowing nutrients from the soil in its first season. Rates depend on soil, crop and goal, and the sensible path is the one we recommend: start with a measured strip or block, compare against your own baseline, then scale what the numbers support. We supply sizing and blending guidance with every order.

How does it pair with wood vinegar?

Every pyrolysis run that makes biochar also condenses wood vinegar, a natural liquid used by growers for seed treatment and plant vigour. The two work at different ends of the plant: biochar improves the soil the roots live in, wood vinegar supports the plant above it. Buying them together from the same process is the practical option for growers who want a natural input program from a single supplier.

How do carbon credits stack on top?

Because biochar carbon stays put for centuries, applying it is certifiable carbon dioxide removal under the IPCC-recognised biochar pathway, roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of biochar. The carbon-removal credit index has traded around 130 to 165 dollars per tonne of CO2, which means the credit can be a serious second line next to the agronomic value, for the grower, the project developer or the offtaker, depending on how a project is structured. Frequently asked questions

What does biochar do in soil?

Its pores hold water and nutrients in the root zone and give microbes a stable habitat, while the carbon itself persists for centuries, so the benefit compounds rather than washing out.

How long does the carbon stay in the soil?

Centuries. Pyrolysis converts labile biomass carbon into a stable form, which is why biochar is an IPCC-recognised durable-storage pathway.

Can I earn carbon credits on soil biochar?

Yes. Roughly 2.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne is certifiable as durable removal, and the credit index has traded around 130 to 165 dollars per tonne of CO2. It stacks on the agronomic value.

Why pair it with wood vinegar?

They come from the same pyrolysis run. Biochar improves the soil the roots live in, wood vinegar supports seed and plant vigour, so many growers apply them together.

How much should I apply?

It depends on soil, crop and goal, and it should be charged with compost or nutrients first. We start from your soil context, recommend a rate and blend, and scale from measured trials.
