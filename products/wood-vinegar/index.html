Wood vinegar, a natural value co-product of pyrolysis.

Wood vinegar, or pyroligneous acid, is the reddish-brown liquid recovered when pyrolysis vapour is condensed. It is a natural organic pesticide, foliar biostimulant and soil conditioner, so the same process that makes biocarbon also yields a high-value agricultural input from what was once a waste stream.

What is wood vinegar?

When biomass is pyrolysed, the vapour given off is condensed into a liquid. The heavy fraction is tar; the light, water-rich fraction that settles out is wood vinegar. It carries acetic acid, polyphenols, esters and hundreds of trace bioactives, which is why a naturally occurring by-product behaves like a multi-purpose organic crop input.

The business case

Wood vinegar turns a fraction that many operators discard into a sellable, high-margin organic input, recovered with no extra feedstock from the same pyrolysis unit that produces biocarbon. For growers it is a natural alternative to synthetic crop chemicals; for operators it is a second product line that improves project economics.

Frequently asked questions

What is wood vinegar, exactly?

The reddish-brown, water-rich aqueous liquid condensed from pyrolysis vapour, about 80 to 90 percent water with acetic acid, polyphenols and 200-plus bioactives. Also called pyroligneous acid.

How do I apply it?

Diluted with water, typically 1:50 up to 1:200 depending on use, then sprayed on crops or soil or added to feed. Follow the product label and local guidance.

Is it an approved organic input?

In several markets it is accepted as an organic crop input, but acceptance varies by country and certifier. Confirm with your local organic certifier.

Can you make it alongside biocarbon?

Yes. It is a co-product of the same pyrolysis process, so a single unit yields both the solid carbon and the liquid input.
