Green hydrogen from waste, not just from the grid.

Pyroltech produces hydrogen from waste. Gasification converts residues into syngas, the gas is cleaned, and the water-gas shift reacts carbon monoxide with steam to make more hydrogen, which is then separated to the purity your process needs. It is a waste-based route that does not compete for renewable electricity or clean water, and it serves the same buyers as any other hydrogen: refineries, ammonia producers and fuel-cell mobility.

How is hydrogen made from waste?

The route runs in three steps. First, gasification converts residues into syngas, a mixture of hydrogen and carbon monoxide. Second, the syngas is cleaned of particulates, tars and contaminants, the same discipline that protects every downstream catalyst. Third, the water-gas shift reacts the carbon monoxide with steam, converting it into additional hydrogen and carbon dioxide, and a separation step then pulls out hydrogen at the required purity. The carbon dioxide leaves as a concentrated stream, which keeps the door open for capture and for negative-emission configurations.

How does it compare with electrolytic hydrogen?

Electrolysis is a fine technology with a hard constraint: it needs large volumes of renewable electricity, and that electricity has other bidders. The waste route sidesteps the queue. Its energy comes from material that someone already has to pay to dispose of, so the economics start with a disposal fee rather than a power bill, and the plant runs on its own gas rather than the grid. For regions with abundant residues and constrained clean power, waste-based hydrogen is often the more practical path to local supply.

Where is the hydrogen used?

Everywhere grey hydrogen is used today. Most of the world's hydrogen goes into refining and chemical synthesis, and those buyers are under growing pressure to cut the carbon intensity of every tonne they consume.

Frequently asked questions

How is hydrogen made from waste?

Gasification produces syngas, the gas is cleaned, and the water-gas shift converts carbon monoxide and steam into additional hydrogen, which is then separated and purified.

How does it differ from electrolytic hydrogen?

Electrolysis needs large volumes of renewable power and clean water. The waste route runs on material that already had to be disposed of, so it does not compete for grid electricity.

Where is it used?

The same places as grey hydrogen: refinery hydrotreating, ammonia and fertilizer synthesis, methanol, and fuel-cell mobility.

The separation step is matched to the end use, from industrial-grade hydrogen through to high-purity mobility grades. Confirmed per project.
